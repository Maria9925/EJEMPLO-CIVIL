Question 2 (*)[R]#

A reservoir of glycerin has a mass of 1200 kg and a volume of 0.952 m3 . Find the glycerin’s weight, mass density, specific weight and specific gravity.

Answer

\(11.77 \; kN \;; 1261 \; kg/m^3 \; ; 12.36 \; N/m^3 \; ; 1.26\)

Hint

The weight is given by the equation: \(W= m \cdot g\)

Mass density (\(\rho\)) is the mass divided by the volume \(\rho=\frac{m}{Vol}\)

Specific weight (\(\gamma\)) is the weight per unit volume and can be calculated as \(\gamma=\rho \cdot g\)

Specific gravity (\(s\)) is the ratio of the fluid’s density to the density of water at 4°C (i.e \(1000 kg/m^3\)):

\(s= \frac {\rho_{fluid}}{\rho_{water_{4c}}}\)

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} m &= 1200 \; \;\textrm{(kg)} \\ \mathrm{Vol} &= 0.95 \; \;\textrm{(m3)} \\ W &= m \cdot g \\&= 1200 \cdot 9.81 \\&= 11772.00 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\ \\ \rho_{glycerin} &= \frac{ m }{ \mathrm{Vol} } \\&= \frac{ 1200 }{ 0.95 } \\&= 1260.50 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\ \\ \gamma_{glycerin} &= \rho_{glycerin} \cdot g \\&= 1260.50 \cdot 9.81 \\&= 12365.55 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\ \\ s_{glycerin} &= \frac{ \rho_{glycerin} }{ \rho_{water} } \\&= \frac{ 1260.50 }{ 1000 } \\&= 1.26 \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 4 (*)#

A certain gas weighs 16.0 N/m3 at a certain temperature and pressure. What are the values of its density, specific volume, and specific gravity relative to air weighing 12.0 N/m3 ?

Answer

\(1.63 \; kg/ mm^3 \; ; 0.61 \; mm^3/kg \; ; 1.33\)

Hint

Specific weight (\(\gamma\)) is the weight per unit volume and can be calculated as \(\gamma=\rho \cdot g\)

Specific volume is the inverse of the density: \(v=\frac {1}{\rho_{gas}}\)

Specific gravity (\(s\)) is the ratio of the fluid’s density to the density of water at 4°C (i.e \(1000 kg/m^3\)):

\(s= \frac {\rho_{fluid}}{\rho_{water_{4c}}}\)

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \gamma_{gas} &= 16 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)} \\ \gamma_{air} &= 12 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)} \\ \rho_{air} &= \frac{ \gamma_{air} }{ g } \\&= \frac{ 12 }{ 9.8100 } \\&= 1.2232 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\ \\ \rho_{gas} &= \frac{ \gamma_{gas} }{ g } \\&= \frac{ 16 }{ 9.8100 } \\&= 1.6310 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\ \\ v &= \frac{ 1 }{ \rho_{gas} } \\&= \frac{ 1 }{ 1.6310 } \\&= 0.6131 \; \;\textrm{(m3/kg)}\\ \\ s_{gas} &= \frac{ \rho_{gas} }{ \rho_{water} } \\&= \frac{ 1.6310 }{ 1000 } \\&= 0.0016 \\ \\ s_{air} &= \frac{ \rho_{air} }{ \rho_{water} } \\&= \frac{ 1.2232 }{ 1000 } \\&= 0.0012 \\ \\ s &= 1.3333 \; \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 6 (**)[R]#

A fluid with a specific gravity of 1.2 moves through a pipe at rate of 2 000 kg every 6 hours. Calculate the mass density, specific weight and volumetric flowrate of the fluid.

Answer

\(1200 \; kg/m^3 \; ; 11 772 \; N/m^3 \; ; 7.716 x 10^ {-5} \; m^3/s\)

Hint

The density of the liquid can be calculated using the specific gravity (\(s\)) equation, which is \(s= \frac {\rho_{fluid}}{\rho_{water}}\).

Specific weight (\(\gamma\)) is the weight per unit volume and can be calculated as \(\gamma=\rho \cdot g\)

The volumetric flow rate is the volume of fluid passing through a given section per unit of time. The equation used is: \(Q= \frac {Vol}{t}\)

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} s_{fluid} &= 1.200 \; \\ m &= 2000 \; \;\textrm{(kg)} \\ t &= 6 \; \;\textrm{(h)} \\ \rho_{fluid} &= s_{fluid} \cdot \rho_{water} \\&= 1.200 \cdot 1000 \\&= 1200.000 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\ \\ \gamma_{fluid} &= \rho_{fluid} \cdot g \\&= 1200.000 \cdot 9.810 \\&= 11772.000 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\ \\ \mathrm{Vol} &= \frac{ m }{ \rho_{fluid} } \\&= \frac{ 2000 }{ 1200.000 } \\&= 1.667 \; \;\textrm{(m3)}\\ \\ Q_{h} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Vol} }{ t } \\&= \frac{ 1.667 }{ 6 } \\&= 0.278 \; \;\textrm{(m3/h)}\\ \\ Q_{s} &= \frac{ \mathrm{Vol} }{ t \cdot 60 \cdot 60 } \cdot 1000 \\&= \frac{ 1.667 }{ 6 \cdot 60 \cdot 60 } \cdot 1000 \\&= 0.077 \; \;\textrm{(L/s)}\\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 8 (*)#

Calculate the specific weight, density and specific volume of the air inside a car tyre at a temperature of 15°C and pumped to an absolute pressure of 3 bar. Given: Rair = 287 m2/(s2.K).

Answer

\( 35.6 \; N/m^3 \; ; 3.63 \; kg/m^3 \; ; 0.28 \; m^3/kg \)

Hint

By using the ideal gas law, the density of air (\(\rho\)) can be calculated as:

\[\rho = \frac{p}{R_{air} \cdot T}\]

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Show code cell outputs

The values of \(R_{air}\), \(T\), and \(p\) are given. We need to convert the temperature units to K and the pressure units to Pa, as required by the ideal gas law.

By using the ideal gas law, the density of air (\(\rho\)) can be calculated as:

\[\rho = \frac{p}{R_{air} \cdot T}\]

where:

  • \(p\) is the absolute pressure of the air

  • \(R_{air}\) is the specific gas constant for air

  • \(T\) is the absolute temperature of the air in Kelvin

Once the density is known, the specific weight (\(\gamma\)) can be calculated using the equation \(\gamma = \rho \cdot g\)

Finally, the specific volume is the inverse of the density \(v =\frac{1}{\rho}\)

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} T_{k} &= 15 + 273.15 &= 288.15 \; \;\textrm{(K)} \\ p_{Pa} &= 3 \cdot \left( 10 \right) ^{ 5 } &= 300000 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)} \\ R_{air} &= 287 \; \;\textrm{(m2/(s2.K))} \\ \rho &= \frac{ p_{Pa} }{ R_{air} \cdot T_{k} } \\&= \frac{ 300000 }{ 287 \cdot 288.15 } \\&= 3.63 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\ \\ \gamma &= \rho \cdot g \\&= 3.63 \cdot 9.81 \\&= 35.59 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\ \\ v &= \frac{ 1 }{ \rho } \\&= \frac{ 1 }{ 3.63 } \\&= 0.28 \; \;\textrm{(m3/kg)}\\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 10 (***)#

a) If water vapour in the atmosphere has a partial pressure of 3500 Pa and the temperature is 30 °C, what is its specific weight?

b) If the atmospheric pressure is 102 kPa, what is the partial pressure of the (dry) air, and its specific weight?

c) What is the specific weight of the atmosphere (air plus water vapour present)?

Given: Rair = 287 m2/(s2.K); R water vapour = 462 m2(s2.K).

Answer

\( a) \; 0.25 \; N/m^3 \)

\( b) \; 11.11 \; N/m^3 \)

\( c) \; 11.36 \; N/m^3 \)

Question 10 (a)

Hint

By using the ideal gas law, the density of air (\(\rho\)) can be calculated as:

\[\rho = \frac{p}{R_{wvapour} \cdot T}\]

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Solution

Show code cell outputs

The values of \(R_{wvapour}\), \(T\), and \(p\) are given. We need to convert the temperature units to K and the pressure units to Pa, as required by the ideal gas law.

By using the ideal gas law, the density of air (\(\rho\)) can be calculated as:

\[\rho = \frac{p}{R_{wvapour} \cdot T}\]

where:

  • \(p\) is the absolute pressure of the air

  • \(R_{wvapour}\) is the specific gas constant for air

  • \(T\) is the absolute temperature of the air in Kelvin

Once the density is known, the specific weight (\(\gamma\)) can be calculated using the equation \(\gamma = \rho \cdot g\)

Replacing the density equation into the specific weight we have: \(\gamma = \frac{g \cdot p}{R_{oxygen} \cdot T}\)

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} p_{Pa} &= 3500 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)} \\ T_{k} &= 30 + 273.15 &= 303.15 \; \;\textrm{(K)} \\ R_{wvapour} &= 462 \; \;\textrm{(m2/(s2.K))} \\ p_{barometer} &= 102000 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)} \\ R_{air} &= 287 \; \;\textrm{(m2/(s2.K))} \\ \gamma_{wvapour} &= \frac{ g \cdot p_{Pa} }{ R_{wvapour} \cdot T_{k} } = \frac{ 9.81 \cdot 3500 }{ 462 \cdot 303.15 } &= 0.25 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 10 (b)

Hint

The partial pressure can be calculated as \(p_{partial}=p_{total}-p_{v}\)

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} p_{air_{Pa}} &= p_{barometer} - p_{Pa} \\&= 102000 - 3500 \\&= 98500 \; \;\textrm{(kPa)}\\ \\ \gamma_{air} &= \frac{ g \cdot p_{air_{Pa}} }{ R_{air} \cdot T_{k} } \\&= \frac{ 9.81 \cdot 98500 }{ 287 \cdot 303.15 } \\&= 11.11 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 10 (c)

Hint

The specific weight of the atmosphere including the water vapour and the air is

\[\gamma_{atm}=\gamma_{air}+\gamma_{water}\]

Solution

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \gamma_{atmos} &= \gamma_{air} + \gamma_{wvapour} \\&= 11.11 + 0.25 \\&= 11.35 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

What pressure in MPa must be applied to water to reduce its volume by 3% under standard sea level atmospheric conditions?

Question 12 (*)[R]#

By what fraction will the volume of a given quantity of mercury at 20°C be reduced if the pressure on it is increased by 100 MPa?

Answer

\(0.38 \; \% \)

Hint

The compressibility of liquids is described by the following equation:

\[\Delta p =-E_v \frac{\Delta v}{v}\]

The bulk modulus \(E_v\) can be found in a table of the physical properties of common liquids at standard sea-level atmospheric conditions.

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Solution

Show code cell outputs

The compressibility of liquids is described by the following equation:

\[ \Delta p =-E_v \frac{\Delta v}{v} \]

Where:

  • \(\Delta p\) is the change in pressure

  • \(E_v\) is the bulk modulus of elasticity

  • \(\Delta v\) is the change in the specific volume

  • \(v\) is the original specific volume

Solving for the reduction of volume \(\frac{\Delta Vol}{Vol}\), we have

\[\frac{\Delta Vol}{Vol}=\frac{-\Delta p}{E_v}\]

Additionally, using a table of the physical properties of common liquids at the standard sea-level atmospheric conditions the Ev is: 26.9x109 N/m2

Show code cell outputs Hide code cell outputs
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \Delta_{p} &= 100000000 \; \;\textrm{(MPa)} \\ E_{v} &= 26200000000.00 \; \;\textrm{(N/m2)} \\ \mathrm{Vol}_{percentage} &= \left( \frac{ \left( - \Delta_{p} \right) }{ E_{v} } \right) \cdot 100 \\&= \left( \frac{ \left( - 100000000 \right) }{ 26200000000.00 } \right) \cdot 100 \\&= -0.38 \; \;\textrm{(%)}\\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Question 13 (***)#

A rigid cylinder with inside diameter of 15 mm contains a column of water 500 mm long. What will the column length be if a force of 2 kN is applied to its top by a frictionless plunger? Assume a bulk modulus of 2.21 GPa.

Answer

\(497 \; mm\)

Hint

The compressibility of liquids is described by the following equation:

\[\Delta p =-E_v \frac{\Delta v}{v}\]

The change in pressure can be calculated using the force and area as:

\[ \Delta p = \frac {\Delta F}{A}\]

The cylinder’s cross-sectional area is fixed, but the column length is changing, modifying the fluid’s volume

Question 2 (**)[R]#

A space 16 mm wide between two large plane surfaces is filled with oil with a viscosity μ = 0.18 N.s/m2. What force F will be required to drag a very thin plate of 0.4 m2 area between the surfaces at a speed of 0.25 m/s if the plate is positioned 5 mm from the bottom surface?

Answer

\(5.24 \; N\)

Hint

The sum of forces on the plate are in equilibrium since it is moving at a constant velocity.

The shear stress on the plate can be calculated from the viscosity equation.

Assume a linear velocity profile

The viscosity force on the plate is the shear stress × area.

The shear stress will be different on the top and bottom of the plate.

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Solution

Show code cell outputs

\[ F-F_{\tau_{1}}-F_{\tau_{2}}=0 \]
\[ F=F_{\tau_{1}}+F_{\tau_{2}} \]

Question 4 (**)[R]#

A square block weighing 1.1 kN and with side lengths of 250 mm slides down a 20 ° incline on a film of oil 6.0 micrometre thick. Assuming a linear velocity profile in the oil, what is the terminal velocity of the block? The viscosity of the oil is 7 mPa.s.

Answer

\(5.16 \; m/s\)

Hint

At terminal velocity, the block is moving at a constant speed, meaning the net force acting on it is zero.

Select axis parallel to incline.

Calculate weight component in \(x\) direction.

Click here for a video explaining the problem

Solution

Show code cell outputs
\[ \sum F_{x}=0\quad;\quad\text{Newton Ⅰ (equilibrium)} \]
\[\begin{split} F-W\sin\theta=0\\ \end{split}\]
\[ \tau A = W\sin\theta \]

Assume linear velocity distribution

\[ \mu\frac{\mathrm{d} u}{\mathrm{d} y}=W\sin\theta \]
\[ \Delta u = \frac{\Delta y W\sin\theta}{\mu} \]
